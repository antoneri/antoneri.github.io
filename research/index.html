Research

In today's connected world, there is a need for efficient mapping and modeling of flow pathways -- for example how information, people, and different species move through social and biological systems. To understand and mitigate seasonal outbreaks, range shifts of species, and other important organizational phenomena of complex systems, researchers across the sciences use tools from network science.

Today researchers rely on one-step models of flows on networks. However, this conventional approach ignores that the flow direction often depend on more than a single step, that is, where the flows come from. Recent evidence suggest that such higher-order information about real flow pathways is critical for capturing all-important phenomena in the dynamics and function of the system. This evidence exposes a serious shortcoming of conventional approaches and raises a major scientific question: How can we comprehend the higher-order effects of flow pathways in a barrage of data to understand the continuously changing organization of social and biological systems?

To address this question, we will capitalize on our pioneering work on mapping flow pathways and a recent breakthrough that copes with noise and scale. This allows us to take advantage of today's data explosion for revealing important organizational structures in complex systems.

The proposed project takes a novel approach to mapping complex systems. Conventional methods shoehorn interaction data about a complex system into a network, which sets a limit on what regularities can be detected. The proposed project breaks this detectability limit by using higher-order modelling and mapping of flow pathways. Going beyond networks makes it possible to take advantage and make use of today's data explosion and create a tool that is to complex reseach what telescopes are to astronomers and microscopes are to biologists.
